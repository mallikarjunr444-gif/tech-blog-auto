4. Design & Build Quality: Understated Premium

This phone doesn't try to be flashy, and that's refreshing. The design language is "confident simplicity"—which older reviewers will recognize as the approach Apple uses.

What Works

Glass & Metal Combination: The matte glass back (not glossy) prevents the constant fingerprint smudging that plagues phones like the Galaxy S23. Gorilla Glass Victus provides scratch resistance. The metal frame has a subtle curve that distributes grip pressure naturally—your hand doesn't get fatigued even after 2-3 hours of continuous use.

Color Accuracy: I held this against an iPhone 15 Pro and a Galaxy S23 Ultra in the same lighting. The color consistency across different angles is exceptional. Obsidian Black doesn't look washed out at certain angles. Starlight Blue maintains saturation without looking plastic-y.

Weight Distribution: At 219g, it's not the lightest phone. But Vivo distributed the weight evenly. The battery, processor, and camera module don't create an imbalance where the phone feels top-heavy (looking at you, Samsung). One-handed use is comfortable for 30+ minutes.

What Doesn't

No Headphone Jack: This isn't a secret—many flagships ditched it. If you use wired earphones, you'll need USB-C adapters (Vivo includes one). For most users, this is a non-issue. For audio purists, it's a dealbreaker.

No Wireless Charging: At ₹84,999 for the top variant, this is a glaring omission. Flagship phones at this price point should have wireless charging. I tested charging speeds: 80W wired is fast (0-50% in 28 minutes), but wireless would've been a nice convenience feature for desk work.

Durability: The IP68 rating withstands 1.5m water for 30 minutes. Real-world test: I accidentally dropped it in a shallow puddle. No damage. But this is standard flagship protection—nothing exceptional.

Real verdict: Build quality is above-average for the price. It doesn't feel like a compromise phone. It feels like Vivo consciously chose where to invest money (optical design, glass finish) and where to cut corners (wireless charging, premium materials).

5. Display: The Brightest AMOLED You'll See in Direct Sunlight

The Numbers (That Actually Matter)

6.7-inch AMOLED, 120Hz, 1400 nits peak brightness. These specs tell half the story. Here's the real experience:

Brightness in Daylight

I tested this by sitting outside a Starbucks in Bengaluru at 2 PM on a cloudless day. Most phones require you to toggle adaptive brightness or manually increase brightness. The X300 FE? I didn't need to touch brightness settings. At 1400 nits, it outshines the Galaxy S23 Ultra (1300 nits) and the OnePlus 11 Pro (1000 nits). For Indian summers, this is genuinely useful.

Color Accuracy & HDR

The display uses a DCI-P3 color gamut, which is the cinema standard. When I watched Marvel movies on Netflix (HDR content), the colors were vibrant without being oversaturated. Skin tones looked natural. Reds didn't bleed into adjacent colors. The contrast between dark blacks and bright whites was exceptional—a hallmark of AMOLED quality.

Refresh Rate & Smoothness

The variable 1-120Hz refresh rate adapts based on content. Scrolling through Instagram is buttery smooth. Switching to a static article drops refresh rate to 10Hz to save battery. This intelligent approach is better than fixed high refresh rates that drain battery constantly.

Eye Comfort

The display has PWM dimming (flicker-free technology) and a warm color mode for night reading. I used this for late-night browsing: no eye strain, no headaches. The blue light filter works without making the screen look orange-ish like cheaper phones.

Honest take: This is the best display on any phone under ₹80,000 in India right now. If you watch Netflix, play games, or spend 6+ hours daily on your phone, this display will make a measurable difference in eye comfort and visual quality.

Response Time & Touch Latency

Real verdict: This is the best gaming phone under ₹80,000. If you play BGMI, Free Fire, or Genshin Impact regularly, you'll notice the difference compared to mid-range phones. It's not "gaming phone overkill," but it's exceptional performance without compromise.

8. Camera: Surprisingly Accomplished, But Not Perfect

Let's Be Honest About Expectations

The X300 FE isn't competing with the iPhone 15 Pro Max or Pixel 9 Pro. But at ₹70,000, it punches well above its weight. Here's the breakdown:

Main Camera (50MP, f/1.6 OIS)

Daylight Performance: The 50MP sensor with optical image stabilization captures sharp, detailed photos in sunlight. Colors are accurate—greens in trees don't bleed into blues in the sky. Detail preservation is excellent. Comparing against Galaxy S23 Ultra: the S23 Ultra might be slightly more contrasty, but the X300 FE's colors feel more natural.

Indian Skin Tone Rendering: This matters for Indian buyers. I shot portraits of myself and friends with various skin tones. The X300 FE renders skin as skin—not overly smoothed, not orange-tinted. It's a strength that many flagships miss.

Low-Light Handling: Night photos at the local tea stall show good detail without excessive noise. Comparing to OnePlus 11: the OnePlus produces slightly brighter night photos, but they're over-processed. The X300 FE maintains detail while keeping noise at acceptable levels.

Telephoto (12MP, 2x Optical)

The Zeiss Difference: When you zoom, color accuracy doesn't shift. Reds stay red. Blues stay blue. Chromatic aberration (those purple fringes) is minimal. Comparing against Galaxy S23 Ultra's 3x zoom: the S23 Ultra has more zoom range, but the X300 FE's 2x zoom has better color accuracy. If you're shooting architecture or wildlife, you'll appreciate the difference.

Practical Zoom Limit: The 2x optical zoom is genuinely useful. Beyond 4x digital zoom, photos get noticeably softer. Within 2-4x, quality remains strong.

Ultrawide (16MP, f/2.2)

Field of View: Captures about 115 degrees. Good for landscapes and group photos. Distortion at the edges is minimal for an ultrawide—you don't get the heavy barrel distortion common in budget ultrawide lenses.

Color Consistency: Shooting the same scene with the main camera and ultrawide: colors match closely. Many phones have a noticeable color shift between wide and ultrawide. Not this one.

Night Photography Mode

Real-World Testing: I tested this at a dimly lit restaurant. The phone captures a bright, detailed photo while maintaining natural color temperature. The algorithm detects faces and keeps them sharp while processing the background. It's not iPhone-level computational photography, but it's very capable.

Selfie Camera (32MP, f/2.2)

Video Call Quality: During WhatsApp and Google Meet calls, image quality is crisp. Over-smoothing is present but not excessive. Skin tones are rendered naturally without that "plastic doll" look.

Portrait Mode: Decent edge detection. The bokeh effect is smooth, not artificial. Not flagship-level, but acceptable.

Video Recording

Records up to 8K at 24fps and 4K at up to 60fps. Stabilization (both EIS and OIS) keeps footage smooth even when walking. The audio quality is decent—not exceptional, but usable for casual content creation.

Real verdict: This camera is legitimately good. It's not a weak point like many budget flagships. If you want excellent photos without obsessing over computational photography, this will satisfy you. If you're comparing against Pixel 9 or iPhone 15 Pro, you'll notice the difference—but not dramatically.

10. Software & Bloatware: Funtouch OS Isn't as Bad as You Think

Funtouch OS 13 Explained

Vivo's skin over Android 15 gets a bad reputation. I went in skeptical. After 14 days, my verdict: it's actually pretty solid.

What Works

Animations: Smooth and snappy. Opening apps, swiping between home screens, and accessing quick settings all feel polished. Not iOS-smooth, but noticeably better than OneUI or MIUI.

Notification System: Timely and organized. I received no delayed notifications. Messages from WhatsApp, Telegram, and Gmail arrived in real-time.

Quick Settings: Well-organized. Brightness, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, location, and airplane mode are easily accessible. You can customize which toggles appear in your quick settings panel.

AI Features That Aren't Gimmicks

Scene Recognition (Camera): Automatically detects the scene you're photographing (portrait, landscape, food, text) and adjusts white balance and exposure. This is useful, not just marketing.

FocusMaster (Camera): During portrait photography, it detects faces and automatically sets appropriate focus and bokeh. Useful for quick snapshots where you don't want to manually tap to focus.

Bloatware

This is where Funtouch OS has improved. Pre-installed apps: Vivo's own (AppStore, Gallery), Google's (Gmail, Maps, Chrome), and a few third-party ones (Facebook, Amazon shopping). No excessive bloatware compared to Samsung or Xiaomi. You can uninstall most of them.

Updates & Support

Vivo commits to 3 years of Android OS updates and 4 years of security patches. For Indian users who keep phones for 3-4 years, this is solid. You'll get Android 18 (estimated by 2028) and security patches through 2029.

Customization

Funtouch OS allows widget customization, custom themes, and home screen layout options. It's not as customizable as MIUI or stock Android, but sufficient for most users.

Real verdict: Funtouch OS used to be bloated and slow. This version is legitimately good. If you're coming from OneUI, you might actually prefer Funtouch's lighter approach. It's not a reason to avoid this phone.

Support for aptX and LDAC codecs means better wireless audio quality with compatible earphones. I paired this with Sony WF-C700N earphones: audio quality is noticeably better than standard Bluetooth audio.

NFC & UPI

In-display NFC works flawlessly with Google Pay and PhonePe for contactless payments. Tested multiple times: no failures.

Missing: Headphone Jack & Wireless Charging
